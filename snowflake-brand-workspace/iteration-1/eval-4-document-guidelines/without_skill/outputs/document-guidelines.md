# Snowflake Brand Guidelines for Internal vs. Customer-Facing Documents

## Overview

When creating documents that reference the Snowflake partnership, the level of brand rigor differs depending on your audience. Internal strategy docs allow more flexibility, while customer-facing proposals must adhere closely to Snowflake's published brand standards.

---

## Internal Strategy Documents

### Logo and Visual Identity

- You may use Snowflake's logo for internal reference, but there is no strict requirement to follow exact clear-space or sizing rules in working drafts.
- Screenshots and informal logo placements are acceptable for internal decks and strategy docs.

### Naming and Terminology

- Use the full name "Snowflake" on first reference; shorthand like "SF" or "Snowflake platform" is acceptable in internal shorthand after that.
- Product names (e.g., Snowpark, Cortex, Snowflake Marketplace, Snowflake Native Apps) can be referenced informally, but try to use the correct current product names to avoid confusion.
- Avoid outdated product names or features that have been renamed or deprecated.

### Tone and Messaging

- You can speak candidly about competitive positioning, integration challenges, and partnership dynamics.
- Internal docs can include frank assessments of Snowflake's strengths and weaknesses relative to alternatives.
- No need to follow Snowflake's official messaging framework -- use whatever framing best serves the internal audience.

### Data and Claims

- You may cite publicly available Snowflake benchmarks, analyst reports, or internal performance data.
- Label any unverified claims or internal estimates clearly so they are not mistaken for official Snowflake statements.

---

## Customer-Facing Proposals

### Logo and Visual Identity

- Always use the official Snowflake logo obtained from their brand resources or partner portal. Do not recreate, recolor, or distort the logo.
- The Snowflake logo features a stylized snowflake icon in their signature blue. Use only the approved color versions (full color on light backgrounds, white on dark backgrounds).
- Maintain adequate clear space around the logo -- generally at least the height of the snowflake icon on all sides.
- Do not place the logo on busy backgrounds or combine it with other logos in a way that implies a merged entity.
- Snowflake's primary brand color is a medium blue (approximately #29B5E8). Use it sparingly and only where appropriate in co-branded materials.

### Naming and Terminology

- Always write "Snowflake" in full -- never abbreviate to "SF" or use informal shorthand.
- Use the trademark symbol (TM) on first reference if required by any co-marketing or partner agreement you have in place.
- Refer to Snowflake products and features by their official, current names. Examples:
  - "Snowflake Data Cloud" (if still the current umbrella term)
  - "Snowpark" (not "Snowflake Spark" or other informal names)
  - "Snowflake Cortex" for AI/ML features
  - "Snowflake Marketplace" for their data marketplace
- Do not use Snowflake's name as a verb (e.g., avoid "we will Snowflake your data").

### Tone and Messaging

- Align with Snowflake's brand voice: technically credible, forward-looking, and confident without being aggressive.
- Position Snowflake as a strategic platform rather than just a database or warehouse.
- Avoid negative comparisons to competitors in co-branded or partner materials.
- Emphasize themes Snowflake values: data collaboration, near-zero maintenance, elastic performance, governance, and cross-cloud availability.

### Co-Branding and Partner References

- If you are an official Snowflake partner, use the appropriate partner badge (e.g., "Snowflake Partner," "Snowflake Premier Partner") and follow the partner badge usage guidelines from the Snowflake Partner Portal.
- In co-branded materials, keep both companies' logos visually balanced -- neither should dominate.
- Include a disclaimer if needed: "Snowflake, the Snowflake logo, and related marks are trademarks of Snowflake Inc. All rights reserved."
- Do not imply endorsement by Snowflake unless you have explicit written approval.

### Data and Claims

- Only cite publicly available Snowflake performance data, case studies, or analyst reports.
- Do not share Snowflake-internal metrics, roadmap details, or NDA-covered information in customer-facing materials.
- If quoting Snowflake statistics (e.g., number of customers, revenue growth), verify the figures are current and sourced from Snowflake's public communications.

### Document Formatting

- Use clean, professional formatting that complements Snowflake's modern aesthetic.
- Avoid cluttered layouts; Snowflake's own materials tend toward generous whitespace and simple, clear data visualizations.
- If including architecture diagrams, use the official Snowflake icons where available (obtainable from their documentation or partner resources).

## Quick Reference Checklist

| Guideline Area | Internal Doc | Customer-Facing Proposal |
|---|---|---|
| Logo usage | Informal OK | Official assets only |
| Naming | Shorthand acceptable after first use | Full official names always |
| Tone | Candid, analytical | Aligned with Snowflake brand voice |
| Competitive mentions | Permitted | Avoid in co-branded materials |
| Data/claims | Internal estimates OK (labeled) | Public sources only, verified |
| Co-branding | Not applicable | Follow partner badge guidelines |
| Trademark notices | Not required | Include where agreements require |
| Visual style | Flexible | Match Snowflake's clean aesthetic |

## Notes

- These guidelines are based on general knowledge of Snowflake's publicly known brand standards as of early 2026. Always check the Snowflake Partner Portal or your partner manager for the most current brand asset files and usage rules.
- If your partnership tier or agreement includes specific co-marketing terms, those terms supersede the general guidance above.
